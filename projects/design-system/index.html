Creating a simple yet impactful design system
Problem at hand

How can I create a design system that is simple, scalable, and understandable by designers and developers?

Project Overview

My Role - Product Designer

When I joined AfriBDC as the sole Product Designer UI/UX, one of my responsibilities was to build a design system from scratch to resolve UI inconsistencies and improve workflow efficiency. Designed as a single source of truth, it brought structure to the product’s visual identity—colors, typography, iconography, and spacing—and introduced a reusable library of UI components, including buttons, forms, navigation menus, and modals. I created detailed documentation with usage guidelines, component specifications, and implementation resources, ensuring seamless collaboration with developers.

Goal

Drive scalable design: Create robust a robust design system that could be easily adopted, maintained, and evolved with future product development.
Unify user experience: Establish visual consistency.
Enhance accessibility: Ensure all visual components met WCAG accessibility starndards.

Discovery and research

When I started, I had no idea how to organize all my components. Luckily, many great companies like Shopify, Apple, and Google have made their design systems public, allowing me to learn from their structure and approach. However, these systems were massive in scope and served more as inspiration than a step-by-step guide for my own context. To build something practical and scalable for my needs, I dove deeper into design methodology and discovered the Atomic Design principles by Brad Frost. This framework — breaking down interfaces into atoms, molecules, organisms, templates, and pages — gave me a clear mental model for structuring components.I also researched my team’s workflow, the types of products we build, and the recurring design challenges we faced. This research confirmed that a modular, hierarchical approach would make our design system easier to scale, maintain, and adopt across projects.

Define & Ideation

Design approach

Looking back at the original problem — How can I create a design system that is simple, scalable, and easy to use? — I realized that consistency and usability would be just as important as visual appeal. This led me to focus on incorporating design tokens to standardize colors, typography, and spacing, ensuring a unified visual language across all components. I also introduced component variants to account for different states and use cases reducing duplication, and instances to ensure that updates to the main component reflects wherever it is used. Accessibility was built into the foundation from the start, with considerations for contrast, legibility, and clear interaction patterns. This approach not only streamlined the design process but also laid the groundwork for a system that could grow without becoming overly complex.

Prototype

Next Steps

As the design system grows, I hope to create more standardized assets that can be reused in many ways. I hope to break down the the components and document them with how the component behaves within the context of any frameworks that the developers are using.
As the design team grows bigger, I hope to collect feedback from a designer’s perspective on how the system has improved their workflow and onboarding. And on the flip side, I’d love to collect feedback on how the design system can be improved to better serve the purposes of the design team (i.e. what’s the best way to document components and display guides and instructions).
As the design system slowly gets implemented, I look forward to seeing if it continues to keep the design consistent. I would love to implement a survey on how the team interacts the design system on a day to day, and how that has increased their work speed and productivity (or not).
